# How do you put a price on a stream of money over time?

Someone offers you `$100` a year for ten years. Someone else offers `$1,000` right now. **Which is worth more?** The naïve answer ("they're both `$1,000`!") is wrong, because of one observation: `$100` a year from now is *less* than `$100` today — you could have invested today's dollar and held more by next year. Reversing that move — *discounting* — turns a future amount into its present-day equivalent. Then you add up all the discounted amounts. *Integration adds money across time. Discounting makes future money smaller before adding it.*

<Lede>**Future money has to be discounted before it can be added.**</Lede>

<Arc kicker="the arc">

<ArcRow n={1} title="A future dollar is not a present dollar">

If today's dollar can be invested at rate `r` and grow to <Formula>1 + r</Formula> in one year (or to <Formula>e^r</Formula> with continuous compounding from the [logarithm module](/en/modules/log)), then `$1` *next year* is equivalent to <Formula>1/(1 + r)</Formula> *today*. That's the simplest possible <Term id="present-value">present value</Term> calculation. Two payments make the same logic compound: `$1` *two years* from now is <Formula>1/(1 + r)²</Formula> today; `$1` *t years* from now is <Formula>1/(1 + r)^t</Formula> today.

The factor <Formula>1/(1 + r)^t</Formula> — or its continuous cousin <Formula>e^(−rt)</Formula> — is the <Term id="discount-factor">discount factor</Term>. It shrinks with `t` (more time, more discount) and shrinks faster as `r` grows (higher interest, more aggressive discount). The whole module is one product and one sum: *future cash × discount factor, summed across all the future moments money arrives.*

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={2} title="A discrete stream — coupons, salaries, rent">

Real <Term id="cash-flow">cash flows</Term> arrive in discrete pieces. A bond pays `$100` every year for `N` years. A salary pays every two weeks. Rent every month. For each piece, compute its present value separately, then add them all up:

<Formula>{"PV = Σ_{i=1}^N  C_i / (1 + r)^(t_i)"}</Formula>

For a constant `C` paid annually for `N` years at rate `r`, the geometric sum has a closed form: <Formula>{"PV = C · (1 − (1+r)^(−N)) / r"}</Formula> — but the *concept* is just "discount each one, add them all up." A spreadsheet does this naturally; the formula is for paper.

The widget shows a `$1/year` stream. At `r = 5%`, the first ten years contribute about `$7.72` of PV; the next ten add about `$4.74` more (much less); past 30 years, additional payments contribute almost nothing. *Distant money discounts away* — a fact that matters when valuing very long streams (mortgages, pensions, perpetuities, eternity).

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={3} title="Continuous compounding — the exponential takes over">

When payments arrive faster — monthly, daily, every second — the discrete sum becomes a continuous integral, and the discount factor <Formula>1/(1+r)^t</Formula> smooths into <Formula>e^(−rt)</Formula>. Why an exponential? Take the log of <Formula>(1 + r/n)^(n·t)</Formula> as compounding frequency `n` grows: <Formula>n·t · log(1 + r/n) → r·t</Formula>, so <Formula>(1 + r/n)^(n·t) → e^(r·t)</Formula>. *The [logarithm module's](/en/modules/log) identity surfaces here directly* — continuous growth and continuous discount are inverses of one another, and both live in the exponential.

The continuous discount factor <Formula>e^(−rt)</Formula> matches the discrete factor at large `n`, but it's *easier* to integrate than to sum, because of the closed form for exponential antiderivatives ([from the integration module](/en/modules/integration)). For pricing real instruments — bonds, dividends, mortgages — both versions are used; the choice depends on whether your payments arrive in lumps or smoothly.

<CodeBlock slug="presentValue" arc="arc3" />

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={4} title="A continuous cash flow — the integral takes over">

A *cash flow rate* <Formula>c(t)</Formula> says: in a tiny time slice <Formula>dt</Formula> at time <Formula>t</Formula>, you receive <Formula>c(t) · dt</Formula> dollars. The undiscounted total over `[0, T]` is the [integral](/en/modules/integration) <Formula>{"∫_0^T c(t) dt"}</Formula>. To get *present* value, discount each tiny slice as it arrives, then add (integrate) — the future money has to be made smaller *before* it can be summed:

<Formula>{"PV = ∫_0^T c(t) · e^(−rt) dt"}</Formula>

For the simplest case — constant cash flow rate `c` over `[0, T]`, constant rate `r` — the integral is closed-form: <Formula>{"PV = (c/r) · (1 − e^(−rT))"}</Formula>. The widget shades this area on its plot; drag <Formula>r</Formula> and <Formula>T</Formula> and watch the answer move. Two limits matter. <Formula>r → 0</Formula>: no discount, <Formula>PV → c · T</Formula> (just the undiscounted total). <Formula>T → ∞</Formula>: a *perpetuity*, <Formula>PV → c / r</Formula> — the famous formula every finance textbook starts with.

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={5} title="The 5/r rule — distant money barely matters">

The closed form <Formula>{"(c/r)(1 − e^(−rT))"}</Formula> hides one of finance's most useful rules of thumb. The factor <Formula>1 − e^(−rT)</Formula> reaches `99%` of its perpetuity limit at <Formula>r·T = ln(100) ≈ 4.6</Formula>, or roughly <Formula>T ≈ 5/r</Formula>. So at `r = 10%`, the perpetuity limit is essentially reached after `50 years`; at `r = 5%`, it takes `100 years`; at `r = 2%`, it takes `250 years`. *Once you are past `5/r`, extending the horizon barely adds anything.*

This is why infinite-life valuations (perpetuities, real estate, sovereign debt) and very-long-life valuations (insurance reserves, climate damage, multi-generation projects) are *not that different at typical discount rates*. The future just isn't worth that much in present-value terms. The whole intergenerational-economics debate is about whether `r` itself should differ across generations — because the answer to "what does a benefit `200 years` from now cost us today?" is almost entirely a choice of `r`, not a choice of `c`.

<Counterexample>
  Same total cash, very different present value. Consider three streams, each totaling{" "}
  <Formula>$120,000</Formula>: (a) <Formula>$1,000</Formula> every month for 10 years; (b)
  <Formula>$120,000</Formula> all at once at year 10; (c) <Formula>$120,000</Formula> all at once
  today. At <Formula>r = 5%</Formula>: PV of (c) is <Formula>$120,000</Formula>, PV of (a) is{" "}
  <Formula>$94,281</Formula>, PV of (b) is <Formula>$72,786</Formula>. *Same nominal total, three
  different prices.* Timing matters as much as magnitude — and the discount rate amplifies the
  difference. Lottery winners "winning <Formula>$10M</Formula>" are usually quoted the
  *undiscounted* sum across 20-30 years; the lump-sum option is always smaller, and that's not
  the lottery cheating — it's the present-value calculation honest.
</Counterexample>

</ArcRow>

<WhyNotTaught>
  Finance courses introduce present value as a list of memorizable formulas — annuities,
  perpetuities, bond prices, mortgage payments — usually before the student has integrated
  anything. Calculus courses introduce integration as area under curves, usually before the
  student has discounted anything. *They are the same arithmetic.* Lemma puts the integral{" "}
  <Formula>{"∫_0^T c(t) e^(−rt) dt"}</Formula> next to the discrete sum so the reader can see
  what the textbook formulas are: special cases of one identity. The annuity formula is *constant
  c, finite T, discrete*; the perpetuity formula is *constant c, T → ∞, continuous*; bond pricing
  is *step-function c at coupon dates, finite T*. None of these need their own theorem — they
  need the same identity with a different `c(t)`.
</WhyNotTaught>
