# How do you put a price on a stream of money over time?

Someone offers you `$100` a year for ten years. Someone else offers `$1,000` right now. **Which is worth more?** The na√Øve answer ("they're both `$1,000`!") is wrong, because of one observation: `$100` a year from now is *less* than `$100` today ‚Äî you could have invested today's dollar and held more by next year. Reversing that move ‚Äî *discounting* ‚Äî turns a future amount into its present-day equivalent. Then you add up all the discounted amounts. *Integration adds money across time. Discounting makes future money smaller before adding it.*

<Lede>**Future money has to be discounted before it can be added.**</Lede>

</Hook>

<ArcRow n={1} title="A future dollar is not a present dollar">

If today's dollar can be invested at rate `r` and grow to $1 + r$ in one year (or to $e^r$ with continuous compounding from the [logarithm module](/en/modules/log)), then `$1` *next year* is equivalent to $1/(1 + r)$ *today*. That's the simplest possible <Term id="present-value">present value</Term> calculation. Two payments make the same logic compound: `$1` *two years* from now is $1/(1 + r)¬≤$ today; `$1` *t years* from now is $1/(1 + r)^t$ today.

The factor $1/(1 + r)^t$ ‚Äî or its continuous cousin $e^(‚àírt)$ ‚Äî is the <Term id="discount-factor">discount factor</Term>. It shrinks with `t` (more time, more discount) and shrinks faster as `r` grows (higher interest, more aggressive discount). The whole module is one product and one sum: *future cash √ó discount factor, summed across all the future moments money arrives.*

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={2} title="A discrete stream ‚Äî coupons, salaries, rent">

Real <Term id="cash-flow">cash flows</Term> arrive in discrete pieces. A bond pays `$100` every year for `N` years. A salary pays every two weeks. Rent every month. For each piece, compute its present value separately, then add them all up:

$PV = Œ£_{i=1}^N C_i / (1 + r)^(t_i)$

For a constant `C` paid annually for `N` years at rate `r`, the geometric sum has a closed form: $PV = C ¬∑ (1 ‚àí (1+r)^(‚àíN)) / r$ ‚Äî but the *concept* is just "discount each one, add them all up." A spreadsheet does this naturally; the formula is for paper.

The widget shows a `$1/year` stream. At `r = 5%`, the first ten years contribute about `$7.72` of PV; the next ten add about `$4.74` more (much less); past 30 years, additional payments contribute almost nothing. *Distant money discounts away* ‚Äî a fact that matters when valuing very long streams (mortgages, pensions, perpetuities, eternity).

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={3} title="Continuous compounding ‚Äî the exponential takes over">

When payments arrive faster ‚Äî monthly, daily, every second ‚Äî the discrete sum becomes a continuous integral, and the discount factor $1/(1+r)^t$ smooths into $e^(‚àírt)$. Why an exponential? Take the log of $(1 + r/n)^(n¬∑t)$ as compounding frequency `n` grows: $n¬∑t ¬∑ \log(1 + r/n) ‚Üí r¬∑t$, so $(1 + r/n)^(n¬∑t) ‚Üí e^(r¬∑t)$. *The [logarithm module's](/en/modules/log) identity surfaces here directly* ‚Äî continuous growth and continuous discount are inverses of one another, and both live in the exponential.

The continuous discount factor $e^(‚àírt)$ matches the discrete factor at large `n`, but it's *easier* to integrate than to sum, because of the closed form for exponential antiderivatives ([from the integration module](/en/modules/integration)). For pricing real instruments ‚Äî bonds, dividends, mortgages ‚Äî both versions are used; the choice depends on whether your payments arrive in lumps or smoothly.

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={4} title="A continuous cash flow ‚Äî the integral takes over">

A *cash flow rate* $c(t)$ says: in a tiny time slice $dt$ at time $t$, you receive $c(t) ¬∑ dt$ dollars. The undiscounted total over `[0, T]` is the [integral](/en/modules/integration) $‚à´_0^T c(t) dt$. To get *present* value, discount each tiny slice as it arrives, then add (integrate) ‚Äî the future money has to be made smaller *before* it can be summed:

$PV = ‚à´_0^T c(t) ¬∑ e^(‚àírt) dt$

For the simplest case ‚Äî constant cash flow rate `c` over `[0, T]`, constant rate `r` ‚Äî the integral is closed-form: $PV = (c/r) ¬∑ (1 ‚àí e^(‚àírT))$. The widget shades this area on its plot; drag $r$ and $T$ and watch the answer move. Two limits matter. $r ‚Üí 0$: no discount, $PV ‚Üí c ¬∑ T$ (just the undiscounted total). $T ‚Üí ‚àû$: a *perpetuity*, $PV ‚Üí c / r$ ‚Äî the famous formula every finance textbook starts with.

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={5} title="The 5/r rule ‚Äî distant money barely matters">

The closed form $(c/r)(1 ‚àí e^(‚àírT))$ hides one of finance's most useful rules of thumb. The factor $1 ‚àí e^(‚àírT)$ reaches `99%` of its perpetuity limit at $r¬∑T = \ln(100) ‚âà 4.6$, or roughly $T ‚âà 5/r$. So at `r = 10%`, the perpetuity limit is essentially reached after `50 years`; at `r = 5%`, it takes `100 years`; at `r = 2%`, it takes `250 years`. *Once you are past `5/r`, extending the horizon barely adds anything.*

This is why infinite-life valuations (perpetuities, real estate, sovereign debt) and very-long-life valuations (insurance reserves, climate damage, multi-generation projects) are *not that different at typical discount rates*. The future just isn't worth that much in present-value terms. The whole intergenerational-economics debate is about whether `r` itself should differ across generations ‚Äî because the answer to "what does a benefit `200 years` from now cost us today?" is almost entirely a choice of `r`, not a choice of `c`.

<Counterexample>

  \$120,000: (a) \$1,000 every month for 10 years; (b)
  \$120,000 all at once at year 10; (c) \$120,000 all at once

  \$94,281, PV of (b) is \$72,786. *Same nominal total, three
  different prices.* Timing matters as much as magnitude ‚Äî and the discount rate amplifies the
  difference. Lottery winners "winning \$10M" are usually quoted the
  *undiscounted* sum across 20-30 years; the lump-sum option is always smaller, and that's not
  the lottery cheating ‚Äî it's the present-value calculation honest.
</Counterexample>

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={6} title="Sensitivity ‚Äî how PV moves when the rate moves">

Up to here, $r$ has been a number *you set*. In practice it is a number that *moves* ‚Äî central banks change it, markets re-price it, your view on it shifts. The natural next question is: **if $r$ shifts by a small amount $\Delta r$, how much does PV move?** That is a question about a [derivative](/en/modules/derivatives) ‚Äî specifically $\partial PV / \partial r$, the slope of PV viewed as a function of $r$.

For the continuous case $PV = \int_0^T c(t) ¬∑ e^{-rt} dt$, differentiating under the integral sign gives

$\partial PV / \partial r \; = \; -\int_0^T t ¬∑ c(t) ¬∑ e^{-rt} dt$

‚Äî the *same integral as PV*, except each cash-flow slice is multiplied by an extra $t$. **Far-future money contributes more to the sensitivity.** Define the **modified duration** $D = -(\partial PV / \partial r) / PV$. Then a small rate move predicts a price move by [linearization](/en/modules/linearization):

$\Delta PV \;\approx\; -D ¬∑ PV ¬∑ \Delta r$

A 10-year zero-coupon bond has $D \approx 10$; a 1% rise in rates predicts about a 10% drop in price. A 2-year bond has $D \approx 2$; the same rate move predicts only about 2%. *Duration is a derivative of price with respect to rate, normalized ‚Äî and it is what the bond market uses to price interest-rate risk every day.*

The same shape runs the **Greeks** in option pricing ‚Äî $\Delta$ is $\partial \text{Price} / \partial S$, $\Gamma$ is the second derivative, $\Theta$ is $\partial \text{Price} / \partial t$, $\mathcal{V}$ is $\partial \text{Price} / \partial \sigma$. Every financial instrument has a family of *sensitivities* ‚Äî partial derivatives of the price with respect to each input. The word *derivative* in "financial derivative" and the word *derivative* in calculus are the same word for a deep reason: **the instrument's price is the derivative of something else, and its risk is captured by its own derivatives.**

</ArcRow>

<WhyNotTaught>
  Finance courses introduce present value as a list of memorizable formulas ‚Äî annuities,
  perpetuities, bond prices, mortgage payments ‚Äî usually before the student has integrated
  anything. Calculus courses introduce integration as area under curves, usually before the
  student has discounted anything. *They are the same arithmetic.* Lemma puts the integral{" "}
  $‚à´_0^T c(t) e^(‚àírt) dt$ next to the discrete sum so the reader can see
  what the textbook formulas are: special cases of one identity. The annuity formula is *constant
  c, finite T, discrete*; the perpetuity formula is *constant c, T ‚Üí ‚àû, continuous*; bond pricing
  is *step-function c at coupon dates, finite T*. None of these need their own theorem ‚Äî they
  need the same identity with a different `c(t)`.
</WhyNotTaught>
