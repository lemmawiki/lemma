# Why does gravity write a parabola, every time?

Throw a ball. Ignore air. Its horizontal motion keeps time — the same speed forever. Its vertical motion loses to <Term id="gravity">gravity</Term> — slower up, faster down. **Two perpendicular stories, neither aware of the other**, and yet their shared trace is a <Term id="parabola">parabola</Term>. Not a special property of parabolas — a recipe. Constant velocity meets constant acceleration; their joint picture is quadratic.

<ArcRow n={1} title="Two motions, one ball">

Once the ball leaves your hand, the only force on it is <Term id="gravity">gravity</Term> — pulling straight down. Nothing pushes it horizontally. So horizontally, it carries on at whatever <Term id="velocity">velocity</Term> you launched it with — uniform, forever, until it lands. Vertically, gravity slows it on the way up, stops it briefly at the apex, and speeds it up on the way down. _The horizontal motion has no idea there's gravity. The vertical motion has no idea there's a horizontal velocity._ Each is a one-dimensional problem; we solve them in parallel and only later combine them into a 2D picture.

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={2} title="Equations of motion">

Split the launch velocity into components: $vₓ = v₀ \cos θ$, $v_y = v₀ \sin θ$. The horizontal component is constant; the vertical component decreases at a rate of $g$ (the constant downward <Term id="acceleration">acceleration</Term>): $v_y(t) = v₀ \sin θ − g·t$. Integrate once and you get position:

```
x(t) = v₀ cos θ · t
y(t) = v₀ sin θ · t − ½ g t²
```

That pair is a [parametric curve](/en/modules/parametric-curves): two functions of a shared time parameter $t$. The _image_ of this curve is the shape on the field; the _parametrization_ is the actual motion in time. Same image can be painted by many parametrizations — we'll see one shortly.

The phrase "integrate once and you get position" is doing real work here. Gravity gives acceleration. [Integrate](/en/modules/integration) acceleration to get velocity; integrate velocity to get position. *The parabola is not magic — it is accumulated change twice.* The constants of integration are fixed by initial conditions: at $t = 0$ the velocity is the launch velocity (no fall yet) and the position is the launch point (no displacement yet). Two integrations, two constants, one closed-form trajectory.

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={3} title="Eliminate time, see the parabola">

Solve $x(t) = v₀ \cos θ · t$ for $t$: $t = x / (v₀ \cos θ)$. Plug into $y(t)$:

```
y(x) = (tan θ) · x  −  g · x² / (2 v₀² cos²θ)
```

That is a quadratic in $x$. The trace of two independent linear processes (in $t$) is necessarily quadratic (in $x$) — and the graph of a quadratic is a <Term id="parabola">parabola</Term>. The parabola is the _image_ of the motion. The motion is the _parametrization_. The image hides the time axis; you can read shape off it, but you cannot tell how fast the ball got there.

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={4} title="What you can read off without solving anything">

Four facts fall out of the formula.

- **Time to peak.** Vertical velocity $v_y(t) = v₀ \sin θ − g·t$ hits zero at $t_peak = v₀ \sin θ / g$. That is when the ball is highest.
- **Time to land.** By symmetry, the down-trip mirrors the up-trip: $t_land = 2 · t_peak$. The widget's stat row shows it.
- **Range.** $R = v₀ \cos θ · t_land = v₀² \sin(2θ) / g$. Maximum at $2θ = 90°$, i.e. launch angle $θ = 45°$. The longest throw is the one split evenly between up and forward.
- **Symmetry.** The peak sits at $x = R/2$. The speed at impact equals the speed at launch (same $vₓ$, equal-magnitude opposite-sign $v_y$). Without drag, the ball loses no energy to the air.

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={5} title="Same image, different motions">

The image-vs-parametrization distinction the [parametric-curves](/en/modules/parametric-curves) module makes precise applies right here. Look at $y(x) = (\tan θ) x − g x² / (2 v₀² \cos²θ)$: the shape of the parabola depends only on $θ$ and the ratio $g/v₀²$. Double both $v₀$ and $g$ together by appropriate factors and you get the _same parabola, traversed in a different time_. A throw on the Moon with the right matching speed paints exactly the same shape as a throw on Earth — but slower, because the Moon's clock for the same shape ticks differently.

That is the same lesson as Bezier curves and as the parametric-curves spike that became a module: the picture is one thing, the motion that paints it is another. Two parametrizations can disagree on every detail except the _image_ they produce.

<Counterexample>
  The parabola is the trajectory you get *when you ignore air*. A baseball thrown at 40 m/s has
  Reynolds number ~10⁵; drag is not a small correction. Compute the same equations with quadratic
  drag $F_d = \tfrac{1}{2} ρ C_d A v²$ for a 145 g ball, and the range at **θ = 45°** drops
  from **~163 m** (vacuum) to **~98 m** (air) — *and* the optimal angle shifts from **45°** to
  **~38°**. The clean parabola is a vacuum object; real outdoor motion is one of those, asymmetric,
  foreshortened.
</Counterexample>

</ArcRow>

</Arc>

<Exercise number={1} tag="time to the peak" noCalculator>

A ball is launched at $v₀ = 20 m/s$, $θ = 30°$, with $g = 10 m/s²$. Find $t_peak$ and the maximum height. Use $\sin 30° = 1/2$.

<Solution>
  $v_y(0) = v₀ \sin 30° = 10 m/s$. Setting $v_y(t) = 10 − 10·t = 0${" "}
  gives **t_peak = 1 s**. Max height{" "}
  $= v_y(0)·t_peak − \tfrac{1}{2} g t_peak² = 10·1 − \tfrac{1}{2}·10·1 = 5 m$. (Same as{" "}
  $v_y(0)² / (2g) = 100/20 = 5 m$.)
</Solution>

</Exercise>

<Exercise number={2} tag="derive the range formula">

Starting from $x(t_land) = v₀ \cos θ · t_land$ and $t_land = 2 v₀ \sin θ / g$, derive $R = v₀² \sin(2θ) / g$. Confirm in the widget that the range is largest at $θ = 45°$.

<Solution>
  $R = v₀ \cos θ · (2 v₀ \sin θ / g) = (2 v₀² \sin θ \cos θ) / g = v₀² \sin(2θ) / g${" "}
  using the double-angle identity $\sin(2θ) = 2 \sin θ \cos θ$. Maximum of{" "}
  $\sin(2θ)$ is 1 at $2θ = 90°$, i.e. **θ = 45°**.
</Solution>

</Exercise>

<Exercise number={3} tag="same image, different time">

Two throws have the same launch angle $θ = 45°$: one with $(v₀, g) = (20, 10)$, the other with $(v₀, g) = (40, 40)$. Show that they paint the _same parabola_ $y(x)$, but the second is _faster_ in time. Which quantity controls the image, and which controls the motion?

<Solution>
  $y(x) = (\tan θ) x − g·x² / (2 v₀² \cos²θ)$ depends only on $θ${" "}
  and $g/v₀²$. For both throws this ratio equals{" "}
  $10/400 = 40/1600 = 0.025$, so same parabola. But{" "}
  $t_land = 2 v₀ \sin θ / g$ evaluates to $\sqrt{2} · 20/10 ≈ 2.83 s$ for
  the first and $\sqrt{2} · 40/40 ≈ 1.41 s$ for the second — half the flight time.
  **Image: $(θ, g/v₀²)$**; **motion: anything else, including absolute v₀ and g.**
</Solution>

</Exercise>

<Exercise number={4} tag="speed at impact">

Without doing any time calculation, argue that the ball's _speed_ at impact equals its launch speed $v₀$. (Same height, no drag.) What about the angle the velocity vector makes with the ground at impact?

<Solution>
  By symmetry of the parabola and the time-reversal symmetry of constant acceleration: at landing,{" "}
  $vₓ$ is unchanged (no horizontal force), and $v_y$ has equal
  magnitude but opposite sign of its launch value. So{" "}
  $|v_impact|² = vₓ² + v_y² = v₀²$ — same speed. Direction: launch was at{" "}
  $+θ$ above the ground, impact is at $−θ$ below — equal angle,
  opposite side.
</Solution>

</Exercise>

<WhyNotTaught>
  Most physics intros open with "objects in projectile motion follow a parabolic path" — stated as a
  fact about *nature*, not as a consequence of two assumptions (no air, constant g). The reader
  files away "parabola" without knowing what they traded for it. Lemma keeps the two motions visible
  — horizontal uniform, vertical free-fall — so the parabola arrives as a *theorem*, not a slogan.
  And the counterexample above admits the obvious: outdoors, the parabola is already wrong.
</WhyNotTaught>
