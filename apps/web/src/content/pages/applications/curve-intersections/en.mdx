# Where do the missing intersections go?

Drag two conics on a screen. Sometimes you see **four** crossings. Sometimes **two**. Sometimes **one**. Sometimes **none**. A physics engine doing collision, a vector tool doing clipping, a font rasterizer doing outline cleanup, a ray tracer doing picking ‚Äî all of them need a _stable_ answer to "how many?" What you see on screen flickers; the math underneath does not.

<Lede>
  [Bezout's theorem](/en/modules/bezout) says the count is always **4** for two conics ‚Äî counted in
  the right number system, on the right plane, with the right multiplicity. The widget below is the
  same one the Bezout module ships with; here it answers a different question: *which subset of
  those 4 is what the screen actually shows?*
</Lede>

<ArcRow n={1} title="What the screen sees vs. what is there">

Two real curves on a 2D plane. The "visible count" is just _the number of points your eye finds at the same pixel_ ‚Äî real coordinates, both finite. That is the count a naive collision detector returns by raster sampling, or a CAD engine returns by approximate root-finding. It depends on where the curves happen to be drifting today and is not stable under tiny perturbations.

Try the widget. The $general$ preset shows 4 real crossings ‚Äî visible count agrees with Bezout. The $tangent$ preset drops to 3 visible ‚Äî turn on _show multiplicity_ and one of those gets a $√ó2$ tag. The $disjoint$ preset shows 0 visible ‚Äî turn on _show complex_ and four open dots appear in the side panel. None of the missing intersections were lost. They just left the real-affine plane.

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={2} title="Three places they hide">

Bezout's repair (covered fully in the [Bezout module](/en/modules/bezout)) tells you exactly where the missing crossings went. Three corrections; three places to look.

- **Tangency.** Two crossings collapsed onto one geometric point ‚Äî the algebra still says two, but the renderer sees one dot. The bookkeeping is <Term id="intersection-multiplicity">intersection multiplicity</Term>: a tangent intersection counts as 2.
- **Off the visible plane.** The intersection points have complex coordinates ‚Äî they exist as solutions of the algebra but cannot be plotted in the real $(x, y)$ grid. Two disjoint circles still intersect at $(3/2, ¬± i \sqrt{5} / 2)$; the screen has nowhere to put those.
- **At infinity.** Some intersections live on the line at infinity. Every two distinct circles, no matter where they sit, share the two _circular points_ $I = [1 : i : 0]$ and $J = [1 : ‚àíi : 0]$ ‚Äî that's two of their four Bezout intersections, off-screen and complex, accounted for whether you like it or not. The fix is the <Term id="projective-plane">projective plane</Term>.

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={3} title="Why a graphics engine cares about the stable count">

An engine that only watches "visible count" is fragile. Two near-tangent circles can have visible-count 0, 1, or 2 depending on a sub-pixel jitter. A renderer that reports "no intersection" one frame and "two intersections" the next is reporting numerical noise as physics. The fix is to compute the _algebraic_ count ‚Äî the one Bezout guarantees ‚Äî and then classify each root.

Practically: solve the y-resultant (a quartic in x), find its 4 complex roots with multiplicity, then label each one as _real-affine_ (visible), _complex_ (off-plane), or _at infinity_ (asymptotic). The visible count is the size of the first bucket. Tangencies are marked by repeated roots, not by "two roots that happen to be very close." A _multiplicity-2 real root_ and _two distinct real roots within Œµ of each other_ are different geometric situations that look identical on screen ‚Äî the algebraic classification keeps them apart.

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={4} title="What the engine actually computes">

The pipeline is short. (1) <Term id="resultant">Eliminate one variable</Term> from the two conic equations to get a single polynomial in the other ‚Äî a quartic when both inputs are degree 2. (2) Find its roots numerically (Durand‚ÄìKerner or similar). (3) Recover the corresponding y-coordinates by linear back-substitution. (4) Tag each intersection as real-affine, complex, or at infinity. The widget above performs steps 1‚Äì4 every time you move a slider.

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={5} title="Where this shows up in a graphics stack">

The same Bezout-and-classify logic, in different costumes, runs in:

- **Collision detection.** Two convex shapes (circles, ellipses, capsules) meet how many times? Bezout gives the upper bound; tangency-with-multiplicity detects glancing contact; "0 real, 4 complex" reports clearly disjoint.
- **Vector clipping.** When you intersect two paths in Illustrator/Figma, the tool computes algebraic intersections of cubic Bezier segments ‚Äî degree 3 √ó degree 3 = up to 9 algebraic, classified into visible / out-of-segment / tangent.
- **Ray‚Äìcurve picking.** A line (degree 1) meets a conic (degree 2) in 2 algebraic points. The mouse picks the first real-affine one along the ray. If both are complex, the click misses.
- **Font outline rasterization.** A horizontal scanline (degree 1) crosses a glyph outline (Beziers up to degree 3) in d¬∑1 = d points. Counting those with multiplicity gives the even-odd fill rule for free.

In every case the surface story is "count and classify"; the deep story is Bezout's theorem and a root-classifier. The shape changes; the count protocol doesn't.

</ArcRow>

<Exercise number={1} tag="line meets circle" noCalculator>

The line $y = mx + c$ meets the unit circle $x¬≤ + y¬≤ = 1$ in how many points, by Bezout? For what range of $(m, c)$ are all of those visible (real-affine), and for what range do the missing ones become complex? Sketch one example of each.

<Solution>
  Bezout: 1¬∑2 = **2**. Substitute to get $(1+m¬≤)x¬≤ + 2mc¬∑x + (c¬≤‚àí1) = 0$;
  discriminant is $4(m¬≤ + 1 ‚àí c¬≤)$. **Two real-affine** when{" "}
  $c¬≤ &lt; m¬≤ + 1$ (the line crosses the disk). **Tangent (multiplicity 2)** when{" "}
  $c¬≤ = m¬≤ + 1$. **Two complex** when $c¬≤ &gt; m¬≤ + 1$ (the line
  misses the circle entirely on the real plane). Same Bezout count of 2 in all three cases.
</Solution>

<Exercise number={2} tag="two circles share infinity">

Take any two distinct circles. Bezout says they meet 4 times. Yet the picture on screen never shows more than 2 real-affine intersections. Where are the other 2 always? (Hint: the <Term id="homogenize">homogenized</Term> equation of any circle, evaluated at the line at infinity $Z = 0$, depends only on the leading term.)

<Solution>
  Homogenize $x¬≤ + y¬≤ + ax + by + c = 0$ ‚Üí{" "}
  $X¬≤ + Y¬≤ + aXZ + bYZ + cZ¬≤ = 0$. At $Z = 0$:{" "}
  $X¬≤ + Y¬≤ = 0$, so $[X : Y : 0] = [1 : ¬± i : 0]$ ‚Äî independent of{" "}
  $a, b, c$. So *every* circle passes through $I = [1 : i : 0]${" "}
  and $J = [1 : ‚àíi : 0]$ at infinity. Two distinct circles share both. Bezout's 4 =
  (‚â§ 2 real-affine) + (2 complex at infinity). When the real-affine count drops below 2,
  multiplicity or complex-affine intersections fill the gap.
</Solution>

</Exercise>

<Exercise number={3} tag="tangent vs near-miss" noCalculator>

An engine has two parameters: an angle $Œ∏$ and a tolerance $Œµ$. As $Œ∏$ sweeps through a tangent configuration, the visible-count goes 2 ‚Üí 1 ‚Üí 0 ‚Üí 1 ‚Üí 2. The "tangent" instant is one configuration; the surrounding "near-tangent" instants are different. State the difference algebraically (one root with multiplicity 2 vs two roots within Œµ), and explain why a robust engine should not dedupe based on raw $Œµ$-distance.

<Solution>
  At the tangent instant, the resultant has a *repeated* real root $x = a$ ‚Äî the
  polynomial factors as $(x ‚àí a)¬≤ ¬∑ q(x)$. Near-tangent has two close but *simple*
  real roots $a ¬± Œ¥$. Both look like "one point" to a tolerance-based deduplicator
  with $Œµ &gt; Œ¥$; but the tangent case has different physics (no transverse
  crossing ‚Äî the curves graze and separate) and different robustness behaviour (stable under
  perturbation in one direction; unstable in the other). Robust engines compute exact discriminants
  when possible, or use interval arithmetic to bound the multiplicity decision ‚Äî never raw distance
  alone.
</Solution>

</Exercise>
