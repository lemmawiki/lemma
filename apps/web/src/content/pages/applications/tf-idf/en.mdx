# Why does Google show those results in that order?

For thirty years the decision rule barely changed. <Term id="tf-idf">TF-IDF</Term> ranks documents by a score that _looks like_ a sum of probabilities — and the log inside it is the _same log_ that runs through the [entropy module](/en/modules/entropy). A rare query term carries many <Term id="bit">bits</Term> of information; a common one carries almost none. **The score learned to ignore stopwords without anyone hard-coding the list.**

<Lede>**Rarity is the signal.** Everything else is consequence.</Lede>

</Hook>

<Arc kicker="the arc">

<ArcRow n={1} title="Counting matches ranks docs by length">

Search $"the cat sat"$. Every doc has $the$. The longest doc has it most. Counting matches ranks documents by how many words they contain — not by how relevant they are. We need a score that knows _which words mean something_.

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={2} title="TF — count, divided by length">

<Term id="term-frequency">Term frequency</Term>:
$tf(t, d) = count(t, d) / length(d)$. The fraction of words in $d$
that equal $t$. Without dividing by length, a long doc would win every comparison
just by repeating every word more often. Length normalization is "level the playing field" — same
evidence per word, regardless of how many words there are.

<CodeBlock slug="tfIdf" arc="arc2" />

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={3} title="IDF — rarity is signal">

<Term id="inverse-document-frequency">Inverse document frequency</Term>:
$idf(t) = \log₂(N / df(t))$, where $N$ is the corpus size and
<Term id="document-frequency">df(t)</Term> counts how many docs contain $t$. Walk
the extremes:

<ul className="m-0 mb-3 list-disc pl-6 text-[16.5px] text-ink-soft [&_li]:my-1.5">
  <li>
    $df = N$ (every doc has it): $\log₂(1) = 0$ bits. Stopword.
    Zero contribution.
  </li>
  <li>
    $df = 1$ (only one doc has it): $\log₂(N)$ bits. Maximum
    signal. The query word *singles out* that doc.
  </li>
</ul>

In the 4-doc widget above: $"the"$ shows up in 3 of 4 docs, so its IDF is $\log₂(4/3) ≈ 0.42$ bits — almost nothing. $"fox"$ shows up in 1 of 4, so its IDF is $\log₂(4/1) = 2$ bits. _The same word "fox" is worth 5× more_ to a query than "the", and nobody hard-coded that.

<CodeBlock slug="tfIdf" arc="arc3" />

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={4} title="Why log? — the same log as in entropy">

The log is not a smoothing trick. Treat $df/N$ as "the probability that a random doc contains $t$". Then

<div className="my-3 rounded-md border border-rule bg-rule-soft px-5 py-4 text-center font-mono text-[15px] text-ink">
  idf(t) = log₂(N / df(t)) = −log₂ P(t) = <Term id="surprise">surprise</Term> of seeing t
</div>

That is exactly the self-information from the entropy module: the bits of <Term id="surprise">surprise</Term> you'd feel if a random doc contained that term. A "surprising" word — appears in few docs — carries many bits. A "boring" word — appears in nearly all of them — carries near zero. _IDF is just self-information wearing a different hat._

The frame is heuristic — it pretends "random doc" is uniform over the corpus and that presence is binary. Real corpora are messier. But the intuition the frame delivers is rigorous, and it's the reason the log isn't going anywhere: rarity _logarithmically_ beats frequency, every time.

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={5} title="Why product (tf × idf)? — borrowing from log-probability">

Independent events have probabilities that [multiply](/en/modules/log); their logs add. So if a doc had a probability for each query term independently, the log of the joint probability would be a clean sum: $Σᵢ −\log P(tᵢ)$. TF-IDF imitates that shape:

<div className="my-3 rounded-md border border-rule bg-rule-soft px-5 py-4 text-center font-mono text-[15px] text-ink">
  score(q, d) = Σ<sub>t ∈ q</sub> tf(t, d) · idf(t)
</div>

**But the imitation is not the thing.** $tf$ is a count, not a probability. Multiplying it by $idf$ is _not_ literally taking the log of a joint probability. TF-IDF is a useful ranking score that _borrows_ the "logs of independent probabilities add" shape — not a real likelihood. Treat it as a heuristic motivated by information theory; that is exactly the right amount of trust to give it.

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={6} title="The vector view — cosine, not Euclidean">

Pin a column for every word in the vocabulary. Each doc becomes a <Term id="vector">vector</Term> of TF-IDF weights — mostly zeros, a few non-zeros where the doc actually uses those words. (This sparse-vector representation is called <Term id="bag-of-words">bag of words</Term>: it keeps frequencies but throws away order.) The query is the same kind of vector. Ranking docs becomes "find the doc-vectors closest to the query-vector".

Why <Term id="cosine-similarity">cosine similarity</Term>, not Euclidean distance? Because doc length should not move the score. Take a doc $d$ and its concatenation with itself, $d ++ d$: the second has every component doubled — a vector pointing the same direction, twice as long. Cosine of the angle between the two: $1$ (identical direction). Euclidean distance: $\|d\|$ (non-zero). _Doubling a doc shouldn't change its meaning_; cosine is the metric that agrees.

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={7} title="Why this became the paradigm — and what came after">

TF-IDF won three races at once. It was _fast_ (sparse vectors, inverted index), _scalable_ (web-sized corpora fit in commodity hardware), and _interpretable_ (every score has a per-term breakdown — exactly what the widget shows). It also produced rankings that were hard to embarrass: the model has one strong assumption (term independence) and a probability-flavored intuition, and the failure modes were familiar.

<Term id="bm25">BM25</Term> = TF-IDF + two empirical patches. (a) *Saturation*: the 5th occurrence
shouldn't count as much as the 1st; $tf$ is replaced by
$(k₁+1)·tf / (k₁+tf)$, saturating around $k₁ ≈ 1.2$. (b) *Length
normalization*: docs longer than the average get their tf damped further. Both patches were tuned on
TREC evaluations in the 1990s and stayed.

The bridge to ML: TF-IDF was the first feature vector for text. Naive Bayes classifiers, logistic regression on text, latent semantic analysis — all stand on this representation. Modern dense neural retrievers (DPR, ColBERT, the dual-encoder family) replaced sparse TF-IDF vectors with dense embeddings, but _they still benchmark against BM25_. A new method that doesn't beat the 30-year-old baseline on a real retrieval task is not a new method. The log is still there.

<Counterexample>
  The independence assumption sometimes lies. A query for $"new york"$ on a TF-IDF
  index returns docs that contain *either* "new" or "york" with high score, including docs about
  *new things* in *York, England*. TF-IDF cannot tell that "new york" is one thing. Phrase matching,
  n-grams, and (eventually) dense embeddings are how search engines patched this — none of them
  removed TF-IDF, they layered on top.
</Counterexample>

</ArcRow>

</Arc>

<Pin>
  **The search score behaves like a sum of bits.** Common words give near zero, rare words give
  many. The log is not a trick — it is the unit of information. But **tf × idf is not literally a
  sum of bits**; it is a heuristic that borrows the shape from log-probability. That borrowed shape
  ran the web for thirty years, and still defines what every neural retriever has to beat.
</Pin>

<Exercise number={1} tag="read the widget">

In the widget, search $the$ with the **raw count** ranking. Which doc wins, and why? Now switch to **TF-IDF**. What happens to the ranking, and what does the IDF panel say about $"the"$?

<Solution>
  Raw count: doc 1 ("the cat sat on the mat") wins with count 2. Under TF-IDF, every doc's score
  collapses toward zero — the IDF of "the" is $\log₂(4/3) ≈ 0.42$ bits, so even doc
  1's contribution is $(2/6) · 0.42 ≈ 0.14$. The widget visibly de-emphasizes the
  "stopword winner". *The score learned to ignore "the" without anyone telling it to.*
</Solution>

</Exercise>

<Exercise number={2} tag="compute by hand · TF-IDF" noCalculator>

For the 4-doc corpus in the widget and query $cat$: compute (a) $tf(cat, d)$ for each doc, (b) $idf(cat)$, (c) the TF-IDF score for each doc. Which doc wins, and is it the longer or the shorter cat-containing doc?

<Solution>
  Lengths: doc 1 = 6, doc 2 = 4, doc 3 = 5, doc 4 = 4. Cat appears in docs 1 and 3, so df = 2 and
  idf = $\log₂(4/2) = 1$ bit. tf values: ${1/6, 0, 1/5, 0}$.
  Scores: ${0.167, 0, 0.2, 0}$. **Doc 3 wins** — the shorter of the two
  cat-docs. Length normalization picks the doc that's "more about cat per word".
</Solution>

</Exercise>

<Exercise number={3} tag="predict · adding a duplicate">

Imagine we add 100 copies of doc 1 ("the cat sat on the mat") to the corpus, growing N from 4 to 104. For the query $cat$: predict (without recomputing) what happens to (a) idf(cat), (b) the score of doc 3 ("cat and dog are friends"), (c) the ranking of doc 3 vs the (now numerous) copies of doc 1.

<Solution>
  df(cat) goes from 2 to 102 (every duplicate also contains cat); N goes 4 → 104. So{" "}
  $idf(cat) = \log₂(104/102) ≈ 0.028$ bits — collapsed to near zero. Doc 3's score:
  tf unchanged, idf collapsed, so total score collapses proportionally. *The ranking flattens* —
  once everything contains "cat", "cat" stops being a useful query word. The corpus determined the
  meaning of the query, not the doc. (This is also why search results from a niche corpus rank
  differently than the same query on the open web.)
</Solution>

</Exercise>

<Exercise number={4} tag="the evil one · zero signal">

Suppose a query word appears in _literally every doc_ in the corpus. Compute its IDF and explain why the score for that word, on every doc, is exactly zero. Then: write a one-sentence explanation of why this matches intuition (rather than being a bug).

<Solution>
  $df = N$, so $idf = \log₂(N/N) = \log₂(1) = 0$. Every term's
  contribution $tf · idf = 0$. The intuition: a word in every doc carries no
  information about *which* doc you want — it's the worst possible answer to the question "which doc
  is this query about?". TF-IDF made that "worst possible" be exactly zero, which is what
  self-information also says: $−\log₂ 1 = 0$ bits of surprise. *Stopwords aren't a
  list — they're the zero set of a function.*
</Solution>

</Exercise>

<WhyNotTaught>
  IR textbooks usually present TF-IDF as a sequence of definitions: tf, then idf, then their
  product, then cosine. The product gets a sentence ("we multiply because that's the formula").
  Lemma keeps the product honest: *tf × idf is not a log probability — it borrows that shape*.
  Hiding that distinction is what makes "why log?" feel like a trick rather than a unit of
  information.
</WhyNotTaught>
