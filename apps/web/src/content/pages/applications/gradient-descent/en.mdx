# A model is wrong. Which knob should move, and by how much?

You have a parameter $w$ and a way to measure how wrong the model is — a <Term id="loss-function">loss</Term> $L(w)$. _Which direction_ should $w$ move to make $L$ smaller? _How far_? Answers: the <Term id="derivative">derivative</Term> tells you the direction; a number called the <Term id="learning-rate">learning rate</Term> tells you how far. Together they make one of the simplest, oldest, most-used recipes in numerical mathematics — and the engine of every modern ML system.

<Lede>
  In the widget below, drag the starting $w₀$ and the $η$ slider,
  then press **step**. Three preset rates: *too small* crawls down, *good* snaps to the minimum in a
  couple of steps, *too big* oscillates and explodes. The boundary between "good" and "explodes" is
  the entire stability theory of the method, in one screen. Gradient descent is the canonical
  example of [optimization](/en/modules/optimization) — five steps that recur in finance and
  calibration too.
</Lede>

<ArcRow n={1} title="Wrongness as a number">

Take the simplest possible model: one parameter $w$, one input $x$, prediction $\hat{y} = w · x$. We want $\hat{y} ≈ y$ for the true value $y$. "Closeness" needs a number; the standard choice is squared error $L(w) = (w·x − y)²$ — zero when the prediction is exact, large when it's far, and (importantly) _smooth_ everywhere so we can take derivatives.

The widget fixes $(x, y) = (2, 6)$, so the loss is $L(w) = (2w − 6)² = 4(w − 3)²$ — a parabola with minimum at $w\_ = 3$. The whole problem reduces to: how do we get from any starting $w₀$ to $3$?

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={2} title="Which direction? — the derivative">

The [derivatives module](/en/modules/derivatives) gives the answer in one line: $L'(w) = 2x(wx − y)$ — the slope of the loss curve at the current $w$. If $L'(w) &gt; 0$, $L$ is rising as $w$ grows; we should make $w$ _smaller_. If $L'(w) &lt; 0$, the opposite. The descent direction is always $−L'(w)$.

In the widget, the green arrow at the current point shows that direction. As $w$ approaches the minimum, the arrow shrinks; right at $w\_$ it has zero length — there's no signal left, because the gradient is exactly zero at the minimum.

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={3} title="How far? — the learning rate">

The direction is settled. The size still isn't. We multiply the gradient by a positive number $η$ (the <Term id="learning-rate">learning rate</Term> · <Dialect concept="learning-rate" />) and subtract:

<pre className="my-2 overflow-x-auto rounded-md bg-rule-soft p-3 font-mono text-[13px] leading-[1.55] text-ink">{`w  ←  w  −  η · L'(w)`}</pre>

Different $η$ shapes the trajectory. Try $η = 0.04$ in the widget: each step shrinks the distance to $w_$ by a constant factor (about 0.68 here), so <Term id="convergence">convergence</Term> is geometric but slow. $η = 0.12$ is near the Newton-optimal $η = 1/L''(w_) = 1/8 = 0.125$ — convergence in two or three steps. $η = 0.27$ is past the boundary; we'll see what happens to it next.

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={4} title="Iterate — the descent loop">

The whole method is just step → step → step until either the gradient is small enough (we're near a minimum) or a budget runs out:

<pre className="my-2 overflow-x-auto rounded-md bg-rule-soft p-3 font-mono text-[13px] leading-[1.55] text-ink">{`for _ in range(steps):
    w = w − η · L'(w)`}</pre>

Five lines of code. That's gradient descent. The widget runs exactly this loop when you press _auto_. Press it with $η = 0.12$ and watch $w$ converge to $3$. Press it with $η = 0.27$ and watch the iterates leave the screen.

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={5} title="Diverge — when η goes too far">

For our quadratic loss, write $u = w − w_$. The update rule becomes $u ← (1 − η · L''(w_)) · u$ — the _distance to the minimum_ gets multiplied by $r = 1 − η · L''(w_)$ at each step. Stable convergence requires $|r| &lt; 1$, i.e. $0 &lt; η &lt; 2/L''(w_)$. For our specific loss, $L''(w) = 8$, so any $η &lt; 0.25$ converges and any $η &gt; 0.25$ diverges.

Above $0.25$, each step overshoots the minimum by _more_ than it corrected for. The signs of $u$ alternate (we land on opposite sides) and the magnitude grows. The widget makes this picture: at $η = 0.27$, the iterates ping-pong and balloon. The fix in real ML is never "trial and error" alone — it's measuring local curvature (second-order methods, Adam-style adaptive rates) and using a schedule that _shrinks_ $η$ as the optimum gets closer.

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={6} title="Why this is everywhere in ML">

Real ML doesn't have one parameter; it has millions or billions. It doesn't have one example; it has millions of them. It doesn't have a clean parabolic loss; it has a high-dimensional, non-convex landscape full of saddles and plateaus. None of that changes the recipe — it just changes what each symbol stands for.

<ul className="m-0 mb-3 list-none space-y-1.5 pl-0 [&_li]:border-l-[3px] [&_li]:border-rule [&_li]:pl-3.5 [&_b]:font-semibold [&_b]:text-ink">
  <li>
    **One parameter → vector.** $w$ becomes a parameter{" "}
    <Term id="vector">vector</Term> $θ$; the gradient becomes a vector of partial
    derivatives. Vector subtraction in place of the scalar update — the same two operations the
    [vectors module](/en/modules/vectors) walks through (add, scale).
  </li>
  <li>
    **One example → mini-batch.** Sum (or average) the loss over a small random sample of examples
    each step. The gradient is now *noisy* — that's $SGD$. The noise is a feature:
    it helps escape bad local geometry.
  </li>
  <li>
    **Clean parabola → real loss surface.** Cross-entropy on a deep network is non-convex. There is
    no single optimum; we settle for "good enough" <Term id="local-minimum">local minima</Term>,
    found by the same descent loop.
  </li>
  <li>
    **Hand-derived gradient → autograd.** The chain rule is mechanical. Modern ML frameworks build a
    computation graph during the forward pass and walk it backward to produce $∇L${" "}
    automatically. The derivative we computed by hand for the toy is the same operation, scaled.
  </li>
</ul>

<Exercise number={1} tag="compute the loss" noCalculator>

With $x = 2$, $y = 6$, and $w = 1$, compute the prediction $\hat{y}$ and the loss $L(w) = (\hat{y} − y)²$.

<Solution>
  $\hat{y} = 1 · 2 = 2$. Off by $(2 − 6) = −4$. Loss{" "}
  $(−4)² = 16$. Verify in the widget by setting $w = 1$.
</Solution>

</Exercise>

<Exercise number={2} tag="gradient by hand" noCalculator>

Use the secant-to-tangent recipe from the [derivatives module](/en/modules/derivatives) to derive $L'(w) = 2x(wx − y)$ from scratch. Then evaluate at $w = 1$ with $(x, y) = (2, 6)$.

<Solution>
  $L(w + h) − L(w) = ((w + h)x − y)² − (wx − y)²$. Expand the squares and subtract:
  the result is $2(wx − y)·xh + x²h²$. Divide by $h$:{" "}
  $2x(wx − y) + x²h$. Let $h → 0$:{" "}
  $L'(w) = 2x(wx − y)$. At $w = 1$:{" "}
  $2 · 2 · (2 − 6) = 4 · (−4) = −16$. Negative — the loss is decreasing as{" "}
  $w$ grows, so descent will push $w$ upward. ✓
</Solution>

</Exercise>

<Exercise number={3} tag="one step by hand" noCalculator>

Starting at $w = 1$ with $η = 0.12$ and gradient $−16$, what is $w$ after one step? After two? You should land near the optimum quickly.

<Solution>
  Step 1: $w = 1 − 0.12 · (−16) = 1 + 1.92 = 2.92$. Recompute gradient:{" "}
  $2 · 2 · (5.84 − 6) = −0.64$. Step 2:{" "}
  $w = 2.92 − 0.12 · (−0.64) = 2.92 + 0.077 ≈ 3.00$. Already at the optimum, two
  steps. $η = 0.12$ is near the Newton-optimal{" "}
  $1/L''(w_) = 1/8 = 0.125$.
</Solution>

</Exercise>

<Exercise number={4} tag="why too-large η explodes">

For our quadratic loss, the update on the displacement $u = w − w\_$ is $u ← (1 − 8η) · u$. Show that $η = 0.27$ gives $|u_n| → ∞$ exponentially in $n$. At what $η$ does the iteration sit at the boundary (constant amplitude, no growth, no decay)? What's special about that $η$?

<Solution>
  $r = 1 − 8 · 0.27 = 1 − 2.16 = −1.16$; $|r| = 1.16 &gt; 1$. So{" "}
  $|u_n| = |r|^n · |u_0| = 1.16^n · |u_0|$ — exponential growth. The boundary is{" "}
  $|r| = 1$: either $r = 1$ (no update at all, only at{" "}
  $η = 0$) or $r = −1$ (perfect oscillation around{" "}
  $w_$), which gives **η = 0.25 = 2/L''(w*)**. Right at this critical{" "}
  $η$, the iterate flips sign every step but never shrinks — the algorithm sits on
  the edge between converging and exploding. Real ML training stays well below this boundary, often
  by 10× or more, especially when curvature is unknown or varies across parameters.
</Solution>

</Exercise>

</Exercises>
