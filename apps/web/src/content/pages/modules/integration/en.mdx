# A speedometer reads. How far have you traveled?

You can't see out the window. You only have the speedometer and a stopwatch. The speed is changing — sometimes 30, sometimes 60, sometimes 0. **What's the total distance?** The intuition is to break the trip into short intervals where the speed is "almost constant," multiply each interval's speed by its duration, and add. That's a Riemann sum, and the *limit* of those sums — as the intervals shrink — is the <Term id="definite-integral">definite integral</Term>. Different name, same operation: change accumulated.

<Lede>
  In the widget below, drag <b>N</b> (the rectangle count) and watch the orange sum approach the
  green exact integral. Pick the **velocity** function and you've recovered the projectile-motion
  story from the other direction: the integral of <span className="font-mono">g·t</span> over time
  is the distance you've fallen.
</Lede>

    $∫_a^b f(x) dx$: the accumulated total of $f$ over{" "}
    $[a, b]$. Limit of Riemann sums:{" "}
    $\lim_(N → ∞) Σ f(x_i) · Δx$. Geometrically, the signed area between the curve
    and the x-axis. The pair operation to the derivative — what differentiation undoes.
  </Fragment>
  <Fragment slot="applies">
    Any time you want a *total* from a *rate*. Distance from velocity, work from force, charge from
    current, expected value from a probability density, area from a height function. Numerically:
    every solver from `scipy.integrate` to PDE codes is a Riemann-style sum with a smarter rule.
  </Fragment>
  <Fragment slot="breaks">
    Discontinuities and unbounded intervals require *improper integrals*. Functions whose
    antiderivative has no closed form ($e^(−x²)$, $\sin x / x$)
    must be evaluated numerically. Higher-dimensional analogs (line, surface, volume integrals,
    measure theory) keep the same intuition but require careful bookkeeping. And: the FTC requires
    the integrand to be continuous on the interval; sharper versions ease this, but the
    one-sentence theorem doesn't apply to every function you can write down.
  </Fragment>
</ToolSpec>

<ArcRow n={1} title="Area first — accumulating a constant rate">

The simplest case is a constant rate. If you drive at $60 km/h$ for $2 h$, you cover $60 × 2 = 120 km$. On a graph of speed-vs-time, that's the area of a rectangle: width × height. The total distance is the *area under the speed curve*, even when the curve isn't constant. The whole module is reframing this trivial case for *non-constant* rates.

For a rate that changes linearly — say $v(t) = g · t$, what gravity gives an object dropped from rest — the area under the line over $[0, T]$ is a *triangle*: $(1/2) · T · (g · T) = (g · T²) / 2$. That's the falling distance. No new physics, no new calculus — just *area*.

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={2} title="Riemann sum — finite bookkeeping">

For a curve that isn't a triangle, you can't read the area off geometry. Instead, *approximate*. Chop the interval $[a, b]$ into $N$ strips of width $Δx = (b − a)/N$. Pick a sample point in each strip — left edge, right edge, or midpoint, your choice — and use that one value as the strip's "constant" height. Add up the strip areas. The result is a <Term id="riemann-sum">Riemann sum</Term>:

$S_N = Σ f(x_i) · Δx$

The widget above shows this directly. Pick $f(x) = x²$ over $[0, 1]$ and slide N upward. At $N = 1$ the rectangle is laughably wrong; at $N = 8$ it's recognizable; at $N = 80$ the gap from the true value $1/3$ is invisible. Different rules (left/right/midpoint) approach the same limit at different speeds — left and right rules at $O(1/N)$, midpoint at $O(1/N²)$. *That's why every numerical integrator picks midpoint or better.*

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={3} title="Definite integral — limit of Riemann sums">

The <Term id="definite-integral">definite integral</Term> is what survives in the limit:

$∫_a^b f(x) dx = \lim_(N → ∞) Σ_{i=1}^N f(x_i) · Δx$

A *single number* — the accumulated total. The notation predates Riemann; the symbol $∫$ is a stretched "S" for "sum," the $dx$ is the descendant of $Δx$ after the limit shrinks it. Reading "$∫_a^b f(x) dx$" as "sum of $f(x)$ times tiny $dx$'s, from $a$ to $b$" is exactly correct intuition; it's also the pre-Riemann intuition Newton and Leibniz used.

The widget's "exact integral" readout is this number, computed in closed form (because we picked easy functions). For most real functions, the limit is taken numerically — and Riemann sums of one form or another are how computers actually compute integrals.

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={4} title="Antiderivative — reverse of the derivative">

Integration meets differentiation. From the [derivatives module](/en/modules/derivatives), differentiating $F(x) = x³$ gives $F'(x) = 3x²$. Reading right-to-left: $3x²$ *has an antiderivative*, namely $x³$. An <Term id="antiderivative">antiderivative</Term> of $f$ is any function $F$ with $F'(x) = f(x)$.

Antiderivatives aren't unique — $x³$ and $x³ + 5$ both differentiate to $3x²$. Any constant $C$ can be added without changing the derivative, which is why textbooks write $F(x) + C$. The set of all antiderivatives of one $f$ is a one-parameter family, all parallel translates of each other.

For polynomials, the rule is mechanical: the derivative of $x^n$ is $n · x^(n−1)$, so the antiderivative of $x^n$ is $x^(n+1) / (n+1)$. *Reverse the formula's bookkeeping.* For $\sin x$ the antiderivative is $−\cos x$; for $e^x$ it's $e^x$ (back where you started). A finite list of these and the chain rule cover most of what one runs into in practice.

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={5} title="Fundamental theorem — area equals reverse-derivative">

The two stories — *area under the curve* and *reverse of derivative* — turn out to be the same story. The <Term id="fundamental-theorem-of-calculus">fundamental theorem of calculus</Term> is the bridge:

$∫_a^b f(x) dx = F(b) − F(a)$

for *any* antiderivative $F$ of $f$. The infinite limit of Riemann sums collapses to two function evaluations and a subtraction. *Most* definite integrals you ever evaluate by hand or in a calculator are computed this way; the Riemann limit is what the integral *is*, but the FTC is how you *do it*.

Try the projectile-motion case. The velocity is $v(t) = g · t$; an antiderivative is $(g · t²) / 2$. By the FTC, the distance fallen between $0$ and $T$ is $(g · T²)/2 − 0 = (g · T²) / 2$ — the same triangle area we read off geometry in arc 1, now derived from the antiderivative. Two routes, one number.

The other half of the theorem — the part that says $(d/dx) ∫_a^x f(t) dt = f(x)$ — is what makes the connection tight in both directions. *Differentiation undoes integration; integration accumulates derivatives.* They are not separate operations sharing a textbook chapter; they are inverses in the same way that addition and subtraction are inverses.

<ArcRow n={6} title="Where this shows up — same accumulator, two pillars">

**Integration adds change across time. In physics that change becomes distance; in finance it becomes present value.** The Riemann-sum picture is the same in both pillars; only the rate being summed differs.

<pre className="my-2 overflow-x-auto rounded-md bg-rule-soft p-3 font-mono text-[13px] leading-[1.55] text-ink">{`physics : velocity is accumulated into distance; force·velocity into energy.
finance : a continuous payment rate is accumulated — discounted — into present value.`}</pre>

[**Projectile motion**](/en/physics/projectile-motion) — fall distance is $\int_0^t v(s)\,ds = \tfrac{1}{2} g t^2$. The velocity $v(s) = g \cdot s$ is the rate; integrating gives the area under it; that area is the distance. Two derivatives turn position into acceleration; two integrations run the ladder back the other way.

[**Terminal velocity**](/en/physics/terminal-velocity) — velocity is no longer linear; it bends toward an asymptote. Distance fallen is still $\int_0^t v(s)\,ds$ — area under the curve, only the curve has shape now. Same accumulator, harder shape.

[**Damped oscillator**](/en/physics/damped-oscillator) — average power input under sinusoidal forcing is $\langle F \cdot \dot{x} \rangle$, an integral over a cycle. The peak — resonance — is exactly where this integral is largest; off-resonance, alternating contributions cancel under the integral sign.

[**Present value**](/en/finance/present-value) — a continuous cash-flow rate $c(t)$ has present value $PV = \int_0^T c(t) e^{-rt}\,dt$. The discount factor $e^{-rt}$ shrinks each future moment before the integral adds them. *Money over time* is structurally identical to *velocity over time*: a rate, accumulated.

**Same machine, different nouns: distance in physics, present value in finance — both are the area under a rate curve, with the rate set by the application.**

</ArcRow>

</Arc>

  **Differentiation asks how fast a quantity is changing. Integration asks how much change has
  accumulated.** Riemann sums are what the integral is. Antiderivatives are how you compute it.
  The fundamental theorem says those two pictures are the same picture — the inverse of the
  derivative.
</Pin>

<Exercise number={1} tag="constant rate by hand" noCalculator>

A car drives at a constant $60 km/h$ for 2 hours. Compute the total distance two ways: (a) by reading the area of the rectangle on a speed-vs-time graph, (b) by computing the antiderivative of the constant function $v(t) = 60$ and applying the FTC.

<Solution>
  (a) Area of a 60 × 2 rectangle = **120 km**. (b) Antiderivative of the constant{" "}
  $60$ is $60 · t$; FTC: $60 · 2 − 60 · 0 = 120$.
  Same answer, different route. *The constant case is the calibration: every more complicated
  integral is "the area when the rate isn't constant," and the FTC reduces it to subtracting two
  values of an antiderivative.*
</Solution>

</Exercise>

<Exercise number={2} tag="Riemann sum by hand" noCalculator>

Compute the midpoint Riemann sum for $f(x) = x²$ over $[0, 1]$ with $N = 4$ rectangles. (Midpoints are $0.125, 0.375, 0.625, 0.875$; $Δx = 0.25$.) Compare to the exact value $1/3 ≈ 0.3333$.

<Solution>
  Heights at midpoints: $0.125² = 0.0156$, $0.375² = 0.1406$,{" "}
  $0.625² = 0.3906$, $0.875² = 0.7656$. Sum:{" "}
  $0.0156 + 0.1406 + 0.3906 + 0.7656 = 1.3125$. Multiply by{" "}
  $Δx = 0.25$: $S_4 = 0.3281$. The exact value is{" "}
  $1/3 ≈ 0.3333$; error $≈ 0.005$, or about{" "}
  $1.5\%$. Already useful at $N = 4$; doubling to{" "}
  $N = 8$ drops the error to roughly $0.4\%${" "}
  (midpoint is $O(1/N²)$).
</Solution>

</Exercise>

<Exercise number={3} tag="antiderivative by reversing the chain">

Compute $∫_0^2 (3t² + 2t + 1) dt$ via the FTC. Also compute it via the widget at $N = 100$ midpoint and confirm the two agree.

<Solution>
  Antiderivative: $F(t) = t³ + t² + t$ (each term reversed by the
  $x^n → x^(n+1)/(n+1)$ rule). FTC:{" "}
  $F(2) − F(0) = (8 + 4 + 2) − 0 = 14$. The widget can't directly run this
  function (we picked three presets), but the code-mode arc 5 does — it agrees to four decimals
  at $N = 10000$ midpoint. *FTC: 14, exact, in two function evaluations. Riemann
  sum: 13.999..., approximate, in 10000 evaluations.* Both correct; one is much faster.
</Solution>

</Exercise>

<Exercise number={4} tag="fall distance from velocity">

A ball is dropped from rest. Gravity gives constant acceleration $g = 9.8 m/s²$, so the velocity after time $t$ is $v(t) = g·t$. How far has it fallen by $t = 3 s$?

<Solution>
  Distance is $∫_0^3 g·t dt = g · t²/2 |_0^3 = (9.8)(9)/2 = 44.1 m$. Equivalently,
  the area of a triangle with base $3$ and height $v(3) = 29.4 m/s$
  is $(1/2)(3)(29.4) = 44.1 m$. Two routes, same number. *This is the projectile-
  motion page's $y(t) = (1/2) g t²$ formula — derived once from kinematics, derived
  again here from "integrate v to get y."*
</Solution>

</Exercise>
