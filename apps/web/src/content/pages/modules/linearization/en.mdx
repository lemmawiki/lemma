# Most equations are hard. Their tangent line is easy.

Every time you say "for small $θ$, $\sin θ ≈ θ$" or "near here, things grow linearly" or "to a first approximation," you are <Term id="linearization">linearizing</Term>. The pattern is so common we stop noticing it. The pendulum clock works because of it. Newton's method is built on it. Gradient descent is built on it. Most engineering is the discipline of _staying in the regime where it holds_.

<ArcRow n={1} title="Why we approximate">

The functions that govern the world are mostly _nonlinear_: a pendulum's $\sin θ$, a transistor's exponential current, a gravitational force's $1/r²$, a neural network's softmax. Solving them in closed form is, in most cases, impossible. So we trade away exactness in a controlled way: pick a point we care about, replace the nonlinear function with the closest linear function in a neighbourhood of that point, and accept that the answer is correct only "near enough."

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={2} title="The tangent line at a point">

At any smooth point $a$, the function $f$ has both a value $f(a)$ and a slope $f'(a)$ (from the [derivatives module](/en/modules/derivatives)). The unique line passing through $(a, f(a))$ with that slope is the <Term id="tangent">tangent line</Term>:

<pre className="my-2 overflow-x-auto rounded-md bg-rule-soft p-3 font-mono text-[13px] leading-[1.55] text-ink">{`L_a(x)  =  f(a)  +  f'(a) · (x − a)`}</pre>

That is the linearization of $f$ at $a$. It matches the function in two ways: $L_a(a) = f(a)$ (same value at the anchor) and $L_a'(a) = f'(a)$ (same slope at the anchor). No other line can claim both. The widget draws this line with the dashed brown stroke; for $f(x) = \sin x$ at $a = 0$, the line is $L_0(x) = 0 + 1 · (x − 0) = x$.

<CodeBlock slug="linearization" arc="arc2" />

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={3} title="Error grows as a square">

For any smooth function, the error $f(x) − L_a(x)$ behaves like a quadratic in the deviation $x − a$. Doubling the deviation roughly quadruples the error; halving it cuts the error to a quarter. This is _quadratic, not linear* — and it is the reason linearization is useful: the gap closes very quickly as you approach the anchor.

Concretely for $\sin$ at $0$: the leading error term is $−x³/6$, so $error / (x − a)²$ drifts slowly with $x$ rather than staying constant — the cubic term dominates here. Other functions ($e^x$, $\sqrt{1 + x}$, $1/(1 − x)$) have a constant $error / (x − a)²$ ratio because their second derivative at the anchor is nonzero. Either way, the rule of thumb is the same: _"small" deviations make linear approximation fine; "large" deviations make it wrong, fast._

<CodeBlock slug="linearization" arc="arc3" />

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={4} title="Where this shows up — one tool, two pillars">

**Linearization is the first honest lie: replace a curved thing by the line that tells the truth nearby.** The lie shows up under different names in different pillars; the math is the same.

<pre className="my-2 overflow-x-auto rounded-md bg-rule-soft p-3 font-mono text-[13px] leading-[1.55] text-ink">{`physics : sin θ ≈ θ near zero
ml      : calibration curve ≈ tangent near one bin`}</pre>

[**The pendulum clock**](/en/physics/pendulum-clock) runs on a single linearization: $\sin θ ≈ θ$ for small angles. The nonlinear ODE $\ddot{θ} = −(g/L) \sin θ$ becomes $\ddot{θ} = −(g/L) θ$ — a linear oscillator with a closed-form sinusoidal solution and a *constant-period* swing. The whole 17th-century clock technology lives inside the small-angle regime where the lie holds, and the page's widget makes that regime visible.

[**Model calibration**](/en/ml/model-calibration) does the same trick on a curve a model produces. Click any bin in the reliability diagram and the widget draws the *tangent* to the calibration curve at that bin's center: locally, $actual(p) ≈ m·p + c$. Two numbers — slope and intercept — describe the gap between confidence and frequency *near that bin*. Slope ≈ 1 means the curve is parallel to the diagonal there (a constant shift); slope ≠ 1 means the gap *changes* with confidence, which a global rotation (temperature scaling) can fix. Same tangent-line tool, completely different use.

The same machine also drives [Newton's method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_method) (linearize, find the line's zero, repeat) and [gradient descent](/en/ml/gradient-descent) (the first-order Taylor approximation says $L(w − η·∇L) ≈ L(w) − η·\|∇L\|²$; if $η$ is small enough that the linearization is trustworthy, the loss decreases — past the ceiling and the linearization lies, which is exactly the explosion at $η = 0.27$ in that page's widget). Newton, gradient descent, the pendulum, and calibration all run the same step: replace the curve with its tangent for as long as the tangent is honest.

<CodeBlock slug="linearization" arc="arc4" />

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={5} title="The catch — only 'almost'">

Every linearization is wrong outside its anchor's neighbourhood. The discipline of using the tool well is the discipline of measuring and respecting that neighbourhood:

- Quote a _bound on the deviation_ for which the linear answer is good enough — not just a slogan that "linearization works."
- Compute or estimate the next-order term and check that it is small (or that its sign won't bite you).
- Stay _inside_ the regime by design: clock escapements force small arcs; ML training schedules shrink the learning rate; control systems stay near operating points; circuit designers bias transistors into the linear region. When you can't stay there, switch to a higher-order method or a nonlinear solver — and accept the cost.

That phrase — "the regime where the lie holds" — is the same one the pendulum page closes on. It is not coincidence; it is the pattern this module names. Every applied-math discipline has a private inventory of such regimes, kept by people who know exactly how far they can lean.

</ArcRow>

</Arc>

<Exercises lang="en">

<Exercise number={1} tag="small-angle by hand" noCalculator>

Linearize $f(x) = \sin x$ at $a = 0$. Use the linearization to estimate $\sin 0.1$. The true value (3 decimals) is $0.0998$. What is the error? About what fraction of $x$ is it?

<Solution>
  $L_0(x) = \sin(0) + \cos(0) · x = x$. So $L_0(0.1) = 0.1$. Error{" "}
  $= 0.1 − 0.0998 = 0.0002$ (about $x³/6 ≈ 0.001/6 ≈ 0.000167$ by
  the cubic-term rule — we also have higher-order correction). The error is about{" "}
  $1/500$ of $x$, well below the $0.5\%$ threshold
  most engineers care about.
</Solution>

</Exercise>

<Exercise number={2} tag="exponential at zero" noCalculator>

Linearize $f(x) = e^x$ at $a = 0$. Estimate $e^0.2$. The true value is about $1.2214$. Compare with $e^0.5$ (true: $1.6487$). What does the relative error pattern look like as the deviation grows?

<Solution>
  $L_0(x) = e^0 + e^0 · x = 1 + x$. So $L_0(0.2) = 1.2$ (true
  1.2214; error $0.0214$, about **1.8%**). And $L_0(0.5) = 1.5${" "}
  (true 1.6487; error $0.1487$, about **9%**). The deviation grew{" "}
  $2.5×$; the error grew $~7×$ — close to the predicted{" "}
  $2.5² = 6.25×$ for a quadratic-error scaling. That's the rule "doubling deviation
  quadruples error" in action.
</Solution>

</Exercise>

<Exercise number={3} tag="Newton step as linearization">

Suppose you want to solve $f(x) = 0$ for some nonlinear $f$. You have a guess $x_n$. Linearize $f$ at $x_n$ and find where *that line* crosses zero. Show that the next guess is $x_(n+1) = x_n − f(x_n) / f'(x_n)$. What breaks this iteration?

<Solution>
  The tangent line at $x_n$ is $L(x) = f(x_n) + f'(x_n)(x − x_n)$.
  Set $L(x) = 0$ and solve for $x$:{" "}
  $x = x_n − f(x_n)/f'(x_n)$. Breaks when $f'(x_n) ≈ 0$ (the
  tangent is nearly horizontal — the next guess flies off to infinity), or when the guess is far
  from a root and the linear approximation lies badly. In practice, Newton needs a starting guess in
  the basin where the linearization is faithful.
</Solution>

</Exercise>

<Exercise number={4} tag="why gradient descent's η has a ceiling">

The first-order Taylor approximation of the loss $L(w + d) ≈ L(w) + ∇L(w) · d$ is honest only for small $\|d\|$. The gradient descent step chooses $d = −η · ∇L$. Use the second-order term to argue why the toy quadratic loss in [/ml/gradient-descent](/en/ml/gradient-descent) has a hard $η$ ceiling at $2/L''(w_)$.

<Solution>
  For a 1D quadratic loss $L(w) = c·(w − w_)²/2$, $L''(w) = c${" "}
  everywhere. Linearization at $w$ says the next step{" "}
  $w − η·c(w − w_)$ moves the displacement $u = w − w_$ by the
  factor $r = 1 − η·c$. Stable convergence requires $|r| &lt; 1$,
  i.e. $0 &lt; η &lt; 2/c = 2/L''(w_)$. Above this ceiling, every step *overshoots*
  the linearization's claim, and the iterates explode. This is exactly the boundary the
  gradient-descent widget shows at $η = 0.25$: the linearization stops being
  trustworthy for the chosen step size.
</Solution>

</Exercise>

</Exercises>
