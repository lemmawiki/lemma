# What is a curve doing right now?

A moving point leaves a trail. **The derivative is not the trail.** It is the arrow the trail wants to become at this instant. Average speed knows two points and divides; instantaneous speed knows only one point, but somehow has a number anyway. That number — what every _rate_ in physics, ML, and engineering ultimately is — comes from forcing the secant line through two points to collapse onto the tangent line at one.

<Lede>
  In the widget below: the orange **secant** through two points on <Formula>y = x²</Formula>. Drag
  **h** toward 0 and the secant rotates onto the brown **tangent**. The slope it converges to is the
  derivative at that point — the curve's instantaneous direction, made into a number.
</Lede>

</Hook>

<ToolSpec>
  <Fragment slot="definition">
    The instantaneous rate of change. <Formula>f'(a) = lim h→0 (f(a+h) − f(a)) / h</Formula>. The
    arrow the trail wants to be at this instant — secant slopes squeezed onto a single tangent slope
    as the second point collapses into the first.
  </Fragment>
  <Fragment slot="applies">
    Wherever a *rate* shows up. Velocity (position → speed), gradient descent (loss → step
    direction), small-angle approximation (sin θ ≈ θ near 0), local linearization in physics, ML,
    control. One machine, four names: slope, velocity, rate, gradient.
  </Fragment>
  <Fragment slot="breaks">
    The limit must exist. Sharp corners (|x| at 0), jumps (step functions), and pathological wiggles
    (Weierstrass nowhere-differentiable function) have no derivative. In ML, hidden
    non-differentiabilities — ReLU at 0, max-pooling, indicator functions — are patched by
    *convention*, not by mathematics; the gradient you compute through them is a choice, not a fact.
  </Fragment>
</ToolSpec>

<ArcRow n={1} title="Δ over Δ — average rate">

You drove from <Formula>x = 1 km</Formula> at <Formula>t = 0 s</Formula> to <Formula>x = 9 km</Formula> at <Formula>t = 4 s</Formula>. Your _average_ speed is <Formula>(9 − 1) / (4 − 0) = 2 km/s</Formula>. That number describes the trip as a whole — but it does not tell you what you were doing at <Formula>t = 2 s</Formula>. The line through <Formula>(0, 1)</Formula> and <Formula>(4, 9)</Formula> on the position graph is a <Term id="secant">secant</Term>, and its slope is the average. _You can compute it without any limit._

<ArcRow n={2} title="Shrink the interval — instantaneous rate">

"What was I doing at <Formula>t = 2 s</Formula>?" requires the secant's two points to merge into one. Pick a fixed anchor <Formula>a = 2</Formula> and a small interval <Formula>h</Formula>; compute <Formula>(f(a+h) − f(a)) / h</Formula> as <Formula>h</Formula> shrinks. For <Formula>f(t) = t²</Formula>:

<pre className="my-2 overflow-x-auto rounded-md bg-rule-soft p-3 font-mono text-[13px] leading-[1.55] text-ink">{`(f(2+h) − f(2)) / h
   = ((2+h)² − 4) / h
   = (4 + 4h + h² − 4) / h
   = 4 + h            ← independent of how big h is, except for the +h tail`}</pre>

As <Formula>h → 0</Formula>, the expression converges to <Formula>4</Formula>. Not "approaches" in some hand-wavy sense — the value _is_ <Formula>4 + h</Formula>, and <Formula>h</Formula> can be made as small as you want. The number that survives in the limit, **4**, is the <Term id="derivative">derivative</Term> of <Formula>t²</Formula> at <Formula>t = 2</Formula>: <Formula>f'(2) = 4</Formula>. Geometrically, the slope of the <Term id="tangent">tangent line</Term> through <Formula>(2, 4)</Formula>.

<ArcRow n={3} title="One machine, three names — slope, velocity, rate">

The recipe — pick two points, compute the secant slope, shrink the interval, take the limit — gives the same kind of number whatever you plug in. If <Formula>f</Formula> is a position-vs-time function, the derivative is <Term id="velocity">velocity</Term>. If <Formula>f</Formula> is a graph of revenue vs price, the derivative is "marginal revenue." If <Formula>f</Formula> is a curve drawn on paper, the derivative is the tangent's slope at each point. _Same operation; different physical or geometric interpretation depending on what was on the axes._

The standard pattern: derivative of <Formula>x^n</Formula> is <Formula>n·x^(n−1)</Formula>. The widget shows it for <Formula>n = 2</Formula>: drag the anchor and read off <Formula>2a</Formula>. The same machinery, applied to <Formula>sin x</Formula>, gives <Formula>cos x</Formula>; applied to <Formula>e^x</Formula>, gives <Formula>e^x</Formula> back; applied to a constant, gives 0. The names of these — "differentiation rules" — are bookkeeping. The single underlying operation is the limit of secant slopes.

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={4} title="Differentiate twice — acceleration">

The derivative of a function is itself a function. You can differentiate it again. For <Formula>f(t) = t²</Formula>: <Formula>f'(t) = 2t</Formula> (a line); <Formula>f''(t) = 2</Formula> (a constant). Two derivatives of position give <Term id="acceleration">acceleration</Term> — the rate of change of velocity, which for free fall is the constant <Formula>−g</Formula>.

That tower — position, velocity, acceleration — is the entire content of "Newton's second law" once you have the derivative as a tool: force is mass times the second derivative of position. Most introductory physics is the algebraic and geometric consequences of this single fact.

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={5} title="Where this module gets consumed">

Two pages already ran on derivatives without naming them. Now they have a place to point at.

- [**Projectile motion**](/en/physics/projectile-motion) — the equations of motion <Formula>x(t) = v₀ cos θ · t</Formula> and <Formula>y(t) = v₀ sin θ · t − ½ g t²</Formula> have derivatives <Formula>vₓ = v₀ cos θ</Formula> (constant) and <Formula>v_y(t) = v₀ sin θ − g t</Formula> (linear, the velocity shown on the projectile widget). Differentiating the _position* gives the _velocity_ directly.
- [**The pendulum clock**](/en/physics/pendulum-clock) — the equation <Formula>θ̈ = −(g/L) sin θ</Formula> is just _two derivatives of θ on the left, equals a function of θ on the right_. The "linearization" trick replaces <Formula>sin θ</Formula> with <Formula>θ</Formula>, leaving <Formula>θ̈ = −(g/L) θ</Formula> — the simple harmonic oscillator equation, which the derivative tool can recognize and solve.

The graph at [/graph](/en/graph) now reflects this: both physics applications consume both <Formula>parametric-curves</Formula> and <Formula>derivatives</Formula>. The module is doing the job a module is supposed to do.

<Exercise number={1} tag="average rate by hand" noCalculator>

For <Formula>f(t) = t²</Formula>, compute the average rate of change over the interval <Formula>[1, 3]</Formula>. Then over <Formula>[1, 1.1]</Formula>. Then over <Formula>[1, 1.001]</Formula>. What pattern do you see?

<Solution>
  <Formula>(9 − 1)/(3 − 1) = 4</Formula>. Then <Formula>(1.21 − 1)/0.1 = 2.1</Formula>. Then{" "}
  <Formula>(1.002001 − 1)/0.001 = 2.001</Formula>. The values are **<Formula>2 + h</Formula>** with{" "}
  <Formula>h = 2, 0.1, 0.001</Formula>. Limit at <Formula>h → 0</Formula> is **2** — the derivative{" "}
  <Formula>f'(1)</Formula>.
</Solution>

</Exercise>

<Exercise number={2} tag="instantaneous rate at t = 3" noCalculator>

For <Formula>f(t) = t²</Formula>, derive <Formula>f'(3)</Formula> from the secant-slope definition. Show every step of the algebra.

<Solution>
  <Formula>
    (f(3+h) − f(3)) / h = ((3+h)² − 9) / h = (9 + 6h + h² − 9) / h = (6h + h²) / h = 6 + h
  </Formula>
  . Now let <Formula>h → 0</Formula>: **f'(3) = 6**. (Same pattern: for any <Formula>a</Formula>,{" "}
  <Formula>(2a)·h + h²</Formula> divided by <Formula>h</Formula> is <Formula>2a + h → 2a</Formula>.
  That is why <Formula>d/dt [t²] = 2t</Formula>.)
</Solution>

</Exercise>

<Exercise number={3} tag="tangent slope from the curve">

The widget shows <Formula>y = x²</Formula>. Without using the formula, just by sliding **a** and shrinking **h**, read off the tangent slope at <Formula>x = 0.5</Formula> and at <Formula>x = 1.5</Formula>. State a rule that fits both numbers.

<Solution>
  At <Formula>x = 0.5</Formula>, the slope reads **1**. At <Formula>x = 1.5</Formula>, it reads
  **3**. The rule: <Formula>m(x) = 2x</Formula>. Confirms <Formula>d/dx [x²] = 2x</Formula> directly
  from the picture, without ever doing the algebraic limit.
</Solution>

</Exercise>

<Exercise number={4} tag="differentiate the projectile" noCalculator>

The vertical position of a projectile is <Formula>y(t) = v₀ sin θ · t − ½ g t²</Formula>. Use the same <Formula>(f(a+h) − f(a)) / h</Formula> recipe to derive <Formula>v_y(t) = dy/dt</Formula>. Confirm the answer matches the projectile widget's **v_y** readout.

<Solution>
  <Formula>y(t+h) = v₀ sin θ · (t+h) − ½ g (t+h)²</Formula>. Subtract <Formula>y(t)</Formula> and
  divide by <Formula>h</Formula>: the <Formula>v₀ sin θ</Formula> term contributes{" "}
  <Formula>v₀ sin θ</Formula>; the <Formula>½ g</Formula> term contributes{" "}
  <Formula>½ g (2t + h) = g t + ½ g h</Formula>; subtract sign and let <Formula>h → 0</Formula>: **
  <Formula>v_y(t) = v₀ sin θ − g t</Formula>**. Exactly the formula the projectile-motion widget
  displays at any <Formula>t</Formula>.
</Solution>

</Exercise>
