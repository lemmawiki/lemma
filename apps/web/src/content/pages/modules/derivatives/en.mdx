# What is a curve doing right now?

A moving point leaves a trail. **The derivative is not the trail.** It is the arrow the trail wants to become at this instant. Average speed knows two points and divides; instantaneous speed knows only one point, but somehow has a number anyway. That number тАФ what every _rate_ in physics, ML, and engineering ultimately is тАФ comes from forcing the secant line through two points to collapse onto the tangent line at one.

  **h** toward 0 and the secant rotates onto the brown **tangent**. The slope it converges to is the
  derivative at that point тАФ the curve's instantaneous direction, made into a number.
</Lede>

<ArcRow n={2} title="Shrink the interval тАФ instantaneous rate">

"What was I doing at $t = 2 s$?" requires the secant's two points to merge into one. Pick a fixed anchor $a = 2$ and a small interval $h$; compute $(f(a+h) тИТ f(a)) / h$ as $h$ shrinks. For $f(t) = t┬▓$:

<pre className="my-2 overflow-x-auto rounded-md bg-rule-soft p-3 font-mono text-[13px] leading-[1.55] text-ink">{`(f(2+h) тИТ f(2)) / h
   = ((2+h)┬▓ тИТ 4) / h
   = (4 + 4h + h┬▓ тИТ 4) / h
   = 4 + h            тЖР independent of how big h is, except for the +h tail`}</pre>

As $h тЖТ 0$, the expression converges to $4$. Not "approaches" in some hand-wavy sense тАФ the value _is_ $4 + h$, and $h$ can be made as small as you want. The number that survives in the limit, **4**, is the <Term id="derivative">derivative</Term> of $t┬▓$ at $t = 2$: $f'(2) = 4$. Geometrically, the slope of the <Term id="tangent">tangent line</Term> through $(2, 4)$.

<ArcRow n={3} title="One machine, three names тАФ slope, velocity, rate">

The recipe тАФ pick two points, compute the secant slope, shrink the interval, take the limit тАФ gives the same kind of number whatever you plug in. If $f$ is a position-vs-time function, the derivative is <Term id="velocity">velocity</Term>. If $f$ is a graph of revenue vs price, the derivative is "marginal revenue." If $f$ is a curve drawn on paper, the derivative is the tangent's slope at each point. _Same operation; different physical or geometric interpretation depending on what was on the axes._

The standard pattern: derivative of $x^n$ is $n┬╖x^(nтИТ1)$. The widget shows it for $n = 2$: drag the anchor and read off $2a$. The same machinery, applied to $\sin x$, gives $\cos x$; applied to $e^x$, gives $e^x$ back; applied to a constant, gives 0. The names of these тАФ "differentiation rules" тАФ are bookkeeping. The single underlying operation is the limit of secant slopes.

<ArcRow n={4} title="Differentiate twice тАФ acceleration">

The derivative of a function is itself a function. You can differentiate it again. For $f(t) = t┬▓$: $f'(t) = 2t$ (a line); $f''(t) = 2$ (a constant). Two derivatives of position give <Term id="acceleration">acceleration</Term> тАФ the rate of change of velocity, which for free fall is the constant $тИТg$.

That tower тАФ position, velocity, acceleration тАФ is the entire content of "Newton's second law" once you have the derivative as a tool: force is mass times the second derivative of position. Most introductory physics is the algebraic and geometric consequences of this single fact.

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={5} title="Where this shows up тАФ same operation, two pillars">

**A derivative is not just a slope. It is local change: how one quantity responds when another is nudged.** The same operation shows up under different names in different pillars; the algebra stays the same.

<pre className="my-2 overflow-x-auto rounded-md bg-rule-soft p-3 font-mono text-[13px] leading-[1.55] text-ink">{`physics : position changes into velocity; velocity into acceleration;
          forces decide those changes.
ml      : loss changes when a parameter moves; the gradient tells
          which way the loss falls.
finance : a price changes when a rate moves; the derivative measures
          the sensitivity тАФ duration for bonds, the Greeks for options.`}</pre>

[**Projectile motion**](/en/physics/projectile-motion) тАФ the equations $x(t) = vтВА \cos ╬╕ ┬╖ t$ and $y(t) = vтВА \sin ╬╕ ┬╖ t тИТ \tfrac{1}{2} g t┬▓$ have derivatives $vтВУ = vтВА \cos ╬╕$ (constant) and $v_y(t) = vтВА \sin ╬╕ тИТ g t$ (linear). Differentiating the *position* gives the *velocity* directly; one more derivative gives the constant $тИТg$ acceleration.

[**The pendulum clock**](/en/physics/pendulum-clock) тАФ the equation of motion $\ddot{╬╕} = тИТ(g/L) \sin ╬╕$ is *two derivatives of ╬╕ on the left, equals a function of ╬╕ on the right*. Replace $\sin ╬╕$ with $╬╕$ (the linearization trick) and you get $\ddot{╬╕} = тИТ(g/L) ╬╕$ тАФ simple harmonic motion, which the derivative tool can recognize and solve.

[**Terminal velocity**](/en/physics/terminal-velocity) тАФ the differential equation $dv/dt = g тИТ k v$ is one derivative on the left, the net force per unit mass on the right. The fixed point тАФ the terminal speed тАФ is found by setting the derivative to zero: $g тИТ k v_t = 0$. *Force balance is exactly "the derivative vanishes here."*

[**Damped oscillator**](/en/physics/damped-oscillator) тАФ the equation $\ddot{x} + 2╬│\dot{x} + ╧ЙтВА┬▓ x = F(t)$ runs two derivatives on the left: a velocity term and an acceleration term. Damping is exactly *the term that multiplies the first derivative*; resonance is *the place where forcing matches the second-derivative geometry*. The whole page is the derivative ladder, twice.

[**Gradient descent**](/en/ml/gradient-descent) тАФ the loss $L(w)$ is a multivariable function; its <Term id="gradient">gradient</Term> $тИЗL$ is the vector of partial derivatives. The descent step $w тЖР w тИТ ╬╖ ┬╖ тИЗL$ uses one derivative per parameter, all at once. The rate at which the loss falls is $\|тИЗL\|┬▓$ тАФ a derivative-derived quantity that determines whether learning is making progress.

[**Present value**](/en/finance/present-value) тАФ the same operation, in finance vocabulary. The price of a bond is $PV(r) = \int_0^T c(t) ┬╖ e^{-rt} dt$, and the *modified duration* $D = -(\partial PV / \partial r) / PV$ is a derivative, normalized тАФ it predicts how much the bond price moves when rates shift. Option Greeks are the same family: $\Delta = \partial \text{Price} / \partial S$, $\Gamma$ the second derivative, and so on. *Financial derivative* and *mathematical derivative* are the same word for a reason.

**Same idea, different nouns: velocity in physics, gradient in ML, sensitivity in finance. In all three the derivative is the local rule for change.**
