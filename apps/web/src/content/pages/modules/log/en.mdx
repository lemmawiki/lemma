# How many times do you multiply 2 to get 1,024?

The answer is 10. That 10 is <Formula>log₂(1,024)</Formula>. **Log is the inverse of exponentiation — it pulls the exponent out of the result.** Most big numbers in nature are built from exponents: cells double, interest compounds, starlight dims as <Formula>1/r²</Formula>, sound spans twelve orders of magnitude. The result you see (1,024 cells, a 100× gain, magnitude-7.2) is rarely the natural parameter. The _exponent_ (how many doublings, how many years) is. Log is the function that recovers it. You already use a special case — <Formula>log₁₀(1,000,000) = 6</Formula> because the number has six zeros. The everyday _digit count is the exponent for base 10_. Generalize the digit count to any base and you have the log. And because exponents add when their bases multiply, log turns multiplication into addition: <Formula>log(a·b) = log(a) + log(b)</Formula>. That one line drives the rest of this page.

<Lede>
  **log = what's the exponent.** Everything else — <Formula>log(a·b) = log(a) + log(b)</Formula>,
  slide rules, log-likelihood, digit-count estimates — is consequence.
</Lede>

</Hook>

<ArcRow n={1} title="The identity that does all the work">

Log is defined by one rule: <Formula>log(a·b) = log(a) + log(b)</Formula>. Pick any base. The rule is the same. (Context picks the base by convention: <Formula>log</Formula> means <Term id="common-log">log₁₀</Term> in engineering, <Term id="natural-log">ln</Term> in ML and statistics, <Formula>log₂</Formula> in algorithms — read by domain when the subscript is omitted.) Every other property falls out of that line. <Formula>log(a/b) = log(a) − log(b)</Formula>: take the rule, replace b with 1/b, done. <Formula>log(aⁿ) = n·log(a)</Formula>: apply the rule n times to <Formula>a · a · … · a</Formula>. <Formula>log(1) = 0</Formula>: from <Formula>log(1·a) = log(1) + log(a)</Formula>. There is no fourth rule because there is no fourth way to combine multiplications. Practically: the <Term id="logarithm">log</Term> of a number tells you _how many factors of the base it is built from_. <Formula>log₁₀(1000) = 3</Formula> because 1000 is three tens, multiplied. Counting factors. That's it.

<ArcRow n={2} title="Same trick, five places">

Exponential quantities scatter across many places — time-growth (<Term id="compound-interest">compound interest</Term>, carbon dating), perceptual compression (decibels), scale-of-nature units (earthquake magnitude), counting information (bits). Same trick each time: set up the equation, take logs, pull the exponent. By hand.

- **Compound interest.** A million won at 7%/year — when does it double? <Formula>1.07ᵗ = 2</Formula> → <Formula>t = log(2) / log(1.07) ≈ 0.301 / 0.0294 ≈ 10.24 years</Formula>. The <Term id="rule-of-72">Rule of 72</Term> (<Formula>72 / 7 ≈ 10.3</Formula>) is this formula sloppily memorized.
- **Carbon-14 dating.** Carbon-14 halves every 5,730 years after death. If 25% remains: <Formula>(1/2)^(t/5730) = 0.25 = (1/2)²</Formula> → <Formula>t = 11,460 years</Formula>. For odd ratios (33%, 17%) only the log expression closes in a single line.
- **Decibels.** <Formula>dB = 10·log₁₀(P/P₀)</Formula>. Conversation 60 dB, rock concert 110 dB → acoustic power differs by <Formula>10⁵ = 100,000×</Formula>. Your ears don't perceive a hundred-thousand-fold gap; hearing is logarithmic in power, and decibels track that compression directly.
- **Earthquake magnitude.** <Formula>E = E₀·10^(1.5·M)</Formula>. Tōhoku 2011 (M 9.0) vs an ordinary large quake (M 7.0): <Formula>E₉ / E₇ = 10^(1.5×2) = 10³ = 1,000×</Formula>. Two units of magnitude, three orders of energy. Natural earthquake energies span 19 orders of magnitude — comparison is hopeless without the compression Richter applies.
- **Bits and binary search.** A 1,024-page dictionary, halving each step → <Formula>log₂(1024) = 10</Formula> steps to find any word. A 32-bit int holds <Formula>2³² ≈ 4.3 billion</Formula> values; identifying N items needs <Formula>log₂(N)</Formula> bits. A deck of cards has <Formula>log₂(<Term id="factorial">52!</Term>) ≈ 226 bits</Formula> of shuffle entropy — 226 yes/no questions to specify a single shuffle exactly.

Five problems, one shape: nature's equation is exponential, take logs both sides, the exponent falls out. The identity from § 1 — multiplication into addition — is doing this work every single time.

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={3} title="Napier and the slide rule (×→+ embodied)">

John Napier published the first log tables in 1614 because astronomers were dying inside, multiplying nine-digit numbers by hand to predict eclipses. His tables let them _look up_ <Formula>log(a)</Formula> and <Formula>log(b)</Formula>, _add_ the two, and _look up_ what number had that log — the answer to <Formula>a·b</Formula> with no multiplication anywhere. Three centuries later, every engineer carried a <Term id="slide-rule">slide rule</Term>: a wooden ruler with two log-spaced scales that _slid past each other_. Aligning **2** on one against **3** on the other physically performed <Formula>log(2) + log(3)</Formula> and showed **6** at the meeting point. **The slide rule is the identity from § 1, made into furniture.** Apollo got to the moon on these.

</ArcRow>

<ArcRow n={4} title="Underflow — and why log-space saves your model">

A <Term id="float32"><Formula>float32</Formula></Term> can hold numbers down to about <Formula>10⁻³⁸</Formula>. Multiply forty probabilities of <Formula>0.1</Formula> and you've crossed it — the result rounds to zero, silently. No exception. No warning. Every <Term id="gradient">gradient</Term> that depended on it dies with it. This isn't a numerical-analysis curiosity; it's why every deep-learning library reports loss as a _sum_, not a product. The fix is the identity from § 1, applied mechanically: take logs the moment a product would otherwise form. <Formula>log(p₁·p₂·…·pₙ) = Σ log(pᵢ)</Formula>. Each <Formula>log(pᵢ)</Formula> is a comfortable negative number; their sum is a comfortable larger negative number. No <Term id="underflow">underflow</Term> can reach you. This is what <Term id="log-likelihood">log-likelihood</Term> is doing, and what <Term id="log-softmax"><Formula>log_softmax</Formula></Term> was built to do. Live in log-space; sums replace products; floats stop lying.

<Exercise number={1} tag="read the graph">

On the Two Stacks widget, set **a = 4**. What value of **b** makes **a·b** land exactly on **100**? Read it off the log axis without computing.

<Solution>
  **b = 25**. log₁₀(4) ≈ 0.60; we need total log = 2; so log(b) = 1.40, i.e. b ≈ 25. Visually, the b
  handle ends up two-thirds of the way between 10 and 100.
</Solution>

</Exercise>

<Exercise number={2} tag="compute by hand · the digit rule" noCalculator>

Without a calculator, give **log₁₀(2,000,000)** using only <Formula>log₁₀(2) ≈ 0.301</Formula>.

<Solution>
  log₁₀(2 · 10⁶) = log₁₀(2) + log₁₀(10⁶) = 0.301 + 6 = **6.301**. The whole exercise is the identity
  from § 1: a product becomes a sum. Memorize log₁₀(2) ≈ 0.301; with that one number you can
  ballpark the log of any number with two digits of precision.
</Solution>

</Exercise>

<Exercise number={3} tag="write the equation · sequence probability">

You evaluate a 50-token sequence; each token has probability ~**0.05**. Write the formula your code should compute, _and_ the formula it should _avoid_. Use <Formula>log(0.05) ≈ −3.00</Formula>.

<Solution>
  *Avoid*: P = 0.05⁵⁰ ≈ <Formula>10⁻⁶⁵</Formula> — zero in float32, almost zero in float64.
  *Compute*: log P = 50·log(0.05) ≈ 50·(−3.00) = **−150**. The product is the same number; the sum
  is the only one your computer can hold.
</Solution>

</Exercise>

<Exercise number={4} tag="compute by hand · Stirling on a napkin" noCalculator>

<Term id="stirling-approximation">Stirling</Term>:
<Formula>log₁₀(n!) ≈ n·log₁₀(n) − n·log₁₀(e)</Formula>, with <Formula>log₁₀(e) ≈ 0.434</Formula>.
Estimate **log₁₀(100!)**. *How many digits* does 100! have?

<Solution>
  100·log₁₀(100) − 100·0.434 = 100·2 − 43.4 = **156.6**. So 100! has about **158 digits** (true:
  158). You estimated a 158-digit number using arithmetic that fits on a napkin. *That is what log
  is for.*
</Solution>

</Exercise>

<Exercise number={5} tag="read the graph · equal log-distance = equal ratio">

On Two Stacks, drag **a** and **b** so that the gap log(b) − log(a) is exactly the gap from log(1) to log(10). What does **b/a** always equal, regardless of where you placed them?

<Solution>
  **b/a = 10**. Equal log-distance ⇒ equal *ratio*, not equal *difference*. This is why an
  exponential curve becomes a straight line on a log axis (see [the Bitcoin Pizza
  chart](/en/finance/bitcoin-pizza)): a constant rate is a constant log-distance per unit time.
</Solution>

</Exercise>

<Exercise number={6} tag="write the equation · logsumexp">

You're given two probabilities p and q, but you only know <Formula>log p</Formula> and <Formula>log q</Formula> (not p, q themselves — they'd underflow). Derive a numerically stable expression for <Formula>log(p + q)</Formula>. (This is the <Term id="logsumexp">_logsumexp_</Term> trick.)

<Solution>
  Let <Formula>M = max(log p, log q)</Formula>. Factor:{" "}
  <Formula>log(p + q) = M + log(e^(log p − M) + e^(log q − M))</Formula>. Both inner exponents are ≤
  0, so their exponentials are ≤ 1 — no overflow, no underflow. This is *the* function in every ML
  library: <Formula>torch.logsumexp</Formula>, <Formula>scipy.special.logsumexp</Formula>. Same
  identity from § 1, used backwards.
</Solution>

</Exercise>

<Exercise number={7} tag="the evil one · 'just multiply'">

A junior says: "<Term id="log-softmax"><Formula>log_softmax</Formula></Term> is just a perf optimization. Mathematically you could just multiply the probabilities — switch to float64 if you're worried." Write a _one-paragraph* rebuttal that holds for both <Term id="float32"><Formula>float32</Formula></Term> and <Formula>float64</Formula>. Then state the single equation that makes log-space work.

<Solution>
  Wrong for both. <Formula>float32</Formula> underflows below <Formula>~10⁻³⁸</Formula>;{" "}
  <Formula>float64</Formula> below <Formula>~10⁻³⁰⁸</Formula>. A 1000-token sequence at p = 0.1 per
  token is 10⁻¹⁰⁰⁰ — zero in *both*. The product literally does not exist in machine arithmetic; the
  sum-of-logs always does. The single equation: <Formula>log(a·b) = log(a) + log(b)</Formula>.
</Solution>

</Exercise>
